# Route Crate Agent Notes

## Design Context

### Users
Route Crate is for independent travelers, couples, and small groups making practical travel decisions before departure and during the trip. They often use the product on mobile, in short sessions, with limited attention and sometimes unreliable connectivity. The core jobs are to check money quickly, keep shared trip costs aligned, prepare basic packing, and recover context on the ground without opening multiple apps.

### Brand Personality
Practical, warm, trustworthy.

The interface should feel calm and competent rather than playful or salesy. It should give users confidence that the product will help them make quick decisions without drama. Brand expression should come from composition, restraint, and tone, not from loud visuals or novelty patterns.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a warm travel-desk aesthetic with paper, utility, and editorial cues. Light mode is the primary expression, with dark mode supported as a quieter extension of the same system. The visual language should feel composed and branded, but still operational.

References implied by the current product direction:
- editorial travel desk
- warm neutral surfaces
- compact utility-first layouts
- selective emphasis for the most important task on each screen

Anti-references:
- generic AI startup dashboards
- purple-blue gradient SaaS aesthetics
- repeated identical card walls across every page
- oversized feature treatments that crowd out dense content
- decorative explanation blocks that do not improve task flow

### Design Principles
1. Decision-first hierarchy: every page needs one obvious focal answer or next action within the first screen.
2. Brand through restraint: use tone, rhythm, and typography to create identity; avoid heavy-handed hero flourishes and redundant surfaces.
3. Utility over theater: travel tools should feel fast, reliable, and scannable before they feel expressive.
4. Dense content needs disciplined framing: content hubs should favor compact grids and clear grouping over oversized spotlight modules.
5. Mobile is not a fallback: layouts, spacing, and controls must hold up in narrow, interruption-prone travel contexts.
